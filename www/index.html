In September 2024, approximately 906,000 acres of California burned amidst wildfires, including more than 17,766 in the Boone Fire near Fresno.

The response included 249 incarcerated firefighters across eight conservation camps. Inmate labor in the crews has existed since 1915.

Despite taking on the same risks as other firefighters, incarcerated servicemen made a fraction of the pay as their coworkers. Upon release, many struggled to find work due to background checks and licensing requirements.

Strong Bars, Weak Pay

By Ganon Evans

California's Prison Industry Authority (CALPIA) offers job opportunities for between 5,000 to 7,000 of the California Department of Corrections' roughly 150,000 inmates. Unlike internal roles like cleaning prisons or running mess halls, CALPIA's Prison Inmate Labor Initiative runs its own workshops and partners with private businesses.

CALPIA runs of its revenue from producing and selling goods and services like furniture, baked goods, shoes, license plates. This report will focus on jobs that CALPIA categorizes as manufacturing, service, or agricultural work because their products end up in the homes of Californians daily.

California has as many inmate workers as several largescale business across the state have civilian employees. Let's see what the actual numbers are.

Inmate workers receive between 35 cents to one dolar an hour in pay for their labor. That's similar to the average wage... in 1900. Inmate workers' pay is deducted for room and board, restitution fines, wage garnishments, commissary accounts, and worker's trust accounts.

The California Prison Industry Authority yields $56.5 million of profit every year, though the profit per inmate per industry varies. For instance, crops are the only industry that operates on a net loss of $28,000 per inmate whereas modular construction yields $486,500 for each of its two inmates working there. The most inmates work in construction, where their labor generates $14,127 of profit per worker annually.

800,000 inmates across the United States are involved in some sort of prison labor initiative. The pay across states lines is similarly poor, with 4 states not even offering any sort of compensation. Let's sat someone worked for one hour as an inmate in the 37 states and a comparable inudstrial labor program in a federal prison. They would earn $13.47, which is less than California's minimum wage of $16.90 for just one hour of labor.

This year, legislation passed to pay California's inmate firefighters the federal minimum wage of $7.25 per hour. That's still less than half of the minimum wage in their own state to combat some of the most destructive natural disasters in modern US history.

Inmates have given up some of their freedoms, but using this as a justification for poverty wages diluted by other living costs in prison itself is unfair. CALPIA has used redivicism reductions as a justification for their industrial programs, but the state auditors have found issues in their calculations since 2010. And while inmates may be gaining new workforce skills, the jail time or a felony often means people eager to work get their resumes thrown in the trash.

If inmate firefighters can have their wages increased by over 700% with one bill, the other several thousand prisoners whose goods and services in California's stores have an opportunity for some more attention too.
